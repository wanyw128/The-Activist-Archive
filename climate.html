Summary

Climate Change

Climate change refers to the once natural changes in the earth’s weather and temperature, which have now been drastically altered by human activities since the 1800s. The burning of fossil fuels, clearing of forests, and rise of industry, have all contributed to the greenhouse gas emissions that have furthered global warming and climate change, causing unprecedentedly dramatic events to occur in Earth’s weather patterns. These climate events include rising sea levels, melting ice glaciers and polar ice, declining biodiversity, intense weather conditions, and more. The climate change movement demands governments and industres to globally and socially address the causes and consequences of the climate crisis.

Climate Justice

Climate justice is a social movement that acknowledges the interrelationship between climate change and environmental justice. This form of activism encourages supporters to solve and connect the climate crisis and the inequity that disadvantages underprivileged communities, which has been accentuated by climate change. More vulnerable groups include communities of color, immigrants, and the low-income class, who experience climate impacts disproportionately compared to others.

Timeline

1824 - Joseph Fourier

Joseph Fourier, a French mathematician and physicist, proposed that the earth had to be insulated by radiated energy that was kept in the atmosphere, similar to a blanket or how light is trapped inside a greenhouse.

1860-1896 - John Tyndall and Svante Arrhenius

Physicist John Tyndall further explored Fourier's greenhouse effect theory with many studies, which revealed that the contents of coal gas (CO2, methane and volatile hydrocarbons) had absorbent qualities, especially CO2. He later suggested that small changes in atmospheric composition, could alter climate.

In 1896, Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius calculated the relationship between CO2 levels in the atmosphere and global temperature levels. He demonstrated a direct proportion to explain past Ice Ages that he believed to have resulted from a decline in volcanic activity, which reduced carbon emissions. The researcher then calculated the outcome of the reverse; if CO2 levels were to be doubled, global temperatures would increase by 5 degrees Celsius.

1938 - Guy Stewart Callendar

Guy Callendar noted that temperatures in the North American and Atlantic region have increased after the Industrial Revolution. He later calculated that doubling carbon dioxide levels could warm Earth's temperatures by 2 degrees Celsius. Up until the 1960s, Callendar pushed his ideas of global warming onto the public. Although he was met by many skeptics, Callendar's efforts helped enable federal projects to more closely monitor CO2 levels.
