Anti Asian and Pacific-Islander Hate

  • Summary
  • Timeline
  • Resources

On March 19, 2020, AAPI Equity Alliance (AAPI Equity), Chinese for Affirmative Action (CAA), and the Asian American Studies Department of San Francisco State University co-founded Stop AAPI Hate, a coalition that responds to the violence and discrimination against Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in the United States after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Throughout American history, conflict within the U.S. and with foreign countries have resulted in senseless prejudice, blaming, and legislative action against U.S. citizens and immigrants who are ethnically related to adversary nations and are minorities. Examples of such injustices are the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 wherein Chinese-Americans were denied eligibility of naturalization due to American economic recession, the Page Act of 1875 that obstructed Chinese women immigration on the stereotype that Chinese women were promiscuous prostitutes who carried sexually transmitted diseases, the legislative exlusion of Indian immigrants following the Chinese Exclusion Act, the incarceration of Japanese-Americans during WWII, and the post-9/11 islamophobia and hate crimes against Muslims. America’s progressive and white-dominated face and the social and political underrepresentation of the AAPI community has consistently contributed to the invisibility of these moments of U.S. history. Anti-AAPI incidents have plagued the daily lives of Asian-Americans after being blamed for COVID-19. The AAPI community has endured racist and xenophobic rhetoric and violence in numerous settings, such as work, the streets, and online communities. Anti-AAPI violence continues to take the lives of innocent Asian-Americans, especially women and the elderly. Despite widespread media coverage and opinions that fixate on the racial aspect of high-profile homicide cases against AAPI, people have found it difficult to locate the main reasons for the increased hostility. While some leaders have tackled the lack of affordable health care and rising vagrancy rates in cities like NYC, others propose the urgent unification of marginalized, colored communities, due to the trend of perpetrators being mostly of Black or Hispanic descent. Protests and organizations have seen activists of various ethnicities and backgrounds fighting for equity and peace. Anti-AAPI Hate “recognizes that in order to effectively address anti-Asian racism, we must work to end all forms of structural racism leveled at Black, Indigenous, and other communities of color.”
