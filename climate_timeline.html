1824: Joseph Fourier

Joseph Fourier, a French mathematician and physicist, proposed that the earth had to be insulated by radiated energy that was kept in the atmosphere, similar to a blanket or how light is trapped inside a greenhouse.

1860-1896: John Tyndall and Svante Arrhenius

Physicist John Tyndall further explored Fourier's greenhouse effect theory with many studies, which revealed that the contents of coal gas (CO2, methane and volatile hydrocarbons) had absorbent qualities, especially CO2. He later suggested that small changes in atmospheric composition, could alter climate. In 1896, Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius calculated the relationship between CO2 levels in the atmosphere and global temperature levels. He demonstrated a direct proportion to explain past Ice Ages that he believed to have resulted from a decline in volcanic activity, which reduced carbon emissions. The researcher then calculated the outcome of the reverse; if CO2 levels were to be doubled, global temperatures would increase by 5 degrees Celsius.

1938: Guy Stewart Callendar

Guy Callendar noted that temperatures in the North American and Atlantic region have increased after the Industrial Revolution. He later calculated that doubling carbon dioxide levels could warm Earth's temperatures by 2 degrees Celsius. Up until the 1960s, Callendar pushed his ideas of global warming onto the public. Although he was met by many skeptics, Callendar's efforts helped enable federal projects to more closely monitor CO2 levels.

1958-1960s: Keeling Curve

Geochemist Charles David Keeling outlined the steady increase in atmospheric CO2 levels since 1958 at Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii. These measurements were compiled and later called the Keeling Curve.

1989: IPCC

In 1989, the United Nations established the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) to provide scientific insights and summaries of climate change statistics and improve climate policies.

1997: Kyoto Protocol

On December 11, 1997, the Kyoto Protocol was adopted, aiming to limit greenhouse gases (GHG) emissions. This policy legally bound 37 industrialized countries and the European Union, who were subjected to an emission reduction of 5% compared to 1990 levels by the year 2012. Although President Bill Clinton had signed the protocol initially, President George W. Bush later announced in March 2001 that the U.S. would not execute it.

November 4, 2016: The Paris Agreement

The Paris Agreement was first discussed and adopted by 196 nations at COP 21 in Paris, on December 12, 2015, and went into effect on November 4, 2016. The Paris Agreement is a legally binding international treaty whose goal is to limit rising global temperatures to below 2 degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels, and aim for an even lesser number of 1.5 degrees Celsius. The world’s largest emitters, China, the United States, the European Economic Area and India, agreed to communicate their climate action plans by 2020. On June 1, 2017, President Donald Trump announced the United States’ redrawal from the agreement, but President Joe Biden would later execute the U.S.’s return to the pact on February 19, 2021.
